Education Reflection

This is a reflection on the education I have recieved throughout my life at all grade levels. If you want a formal report of this, check my resume.

Temple Beth Shalom

Growing up, I went to schools all on Miami Beach.

The first school I ever attended was Pre-School at Temple Beth Shalom (TBS)! There we would learn about Judaism (history, torah lessons, etc.) and other basic things like to count, read (kind of lol), sing (we sang a LOT), play, and clean up! We also had outside recess and inside recess. This is where I would make some of my first friends (some of which I am still great friends with today). If you are interested in learning about my friends and family, check my social tab (if I make one!).

Outside recess entailed riding little tricycles, playing tag, hide and seek, make believe, and so much more. Outside was always so nice too as when I was younger, Miami Beach was not so dang HOT! It is so much hotter now than it was when I was a kid, it's crazy!! I can clearly remember the late morning breeze under the shade at the playground. Ahhh, those times were sweet and full of wonder. No stress in sight!

Inside recess entailed playing with Hot Wheels, multi-colored magnetic triangles that could be used to make tons of cool shapes and objects, playing with Play-Doh (NOT EATING IT!!!), and doing arts and crafts like painting and drawing. At the end of each inside recess session, the teacher would play on a speaker the "Clean Up Song" and we would all sing together and clean everything up.

After recess was over, we would all take a nap on these gym mats. In hindsight, it was rather weird but hey, extra sleep never hurt.

Throughout the year, we collectively worked on a class project so that we could present it to our parents at one big event. One year, we made pillows. Another year, we made a solar system and turned the lights off and shined lights on it so we could have our own planetarium. That was my favorite. I still remember how much fun it was presenting it all!

North Beach Elementary

After Temple Beth Shalom, I attended North Beach Elementary School for six years (Kindergarten-5th grade). Funny enough, this school was across the street from TBS (no like actually just a 10 second walk). This school, painted in an interesting color....ahem...Skin Color Beige. I mean I made that name up but I've got nothing else. Look at the picture I put on here, you'll see what I mean...

In Kindergarten, my teacher was Mrs. West, who I thought was the devil. She would always yell at us if we went slightly out of line. She was known around the school as the strictest teacher there. Many years later, I realized that she was more than a normal teacher and we were just rowdy kids. In this class, I would really meet the founding of the friend group I still am part of today. Funny enough, this is also where I got my nickname, Jonny. See, there was another Jonathan in the class, and we could not decide who to call Jonny, so we did rock-paper-scissors, and I lost so I became Jonny (I think I really won because it’s an awesome name).

In 1st grade, my teacher was Mrs. Gonzalez, who was a sweet lady that always took care of me and my siblings. I don't have much else to say here except I had lots of fun in her class and learned a lot. I specifically remember enjoying her science portion the most as we learned about geology and the different types of rocks, which I thought was cool as heck!

In 2nd grade, my teacher was Mr. Diaz. He was a chiller but I honestly do not remember much about him or his class except that I got glasses that year.

In 3rd grade, my teacher was Mrs. Fontanelles. I have no clue how to spell her name at all but she was a younger teacher that taught math well (learned my times tables there) and I also do not remember much else about her except for this one story: my friends and I would make heinous concoctions at lunch and one time we filled a water bottle (with some of the water left in it) full of Fruit Roll-Ups, pretzel sticks, Oreo pieces, and so much more. It looked like an aquarium when we were done. But unfortunately, a girl in our class reported us to Mrs. Fontanelles and she reamed into us about how immature we are, that we should be suspended, and more. This was a complete overreaction over just a water bottle full of scrap lunch food, but it did make for a funny memory.

In 4th grade, my teacher was Mrs. Pena. She was a nice teacher, but the year was rather lack-luster. I remember that this was the first class that I learned how to write essays and focused on my grammar. If you had good grades at the end of each quarter, she would host a pizza party with a movie and candy and those were always fun.

In 5th grade, my teacher was Mrs. Green. She was strict but caring and wanted to make us be the best students we could be. I remember one time I was the only student in the class to get an A on a math test, and that was pretty cool. I remember coming back from PE and always being sweaty and icky before our math session. I do not know how we used to go from being super sweaty to just sitting down in a hot classroom for hours.

Nautilus Middle

For middle school, I went to Nautilus Middle School, which was down the road from my other two schools. Middle school was quite a time. So many unique experiences and new friends made. Going from one teacher to many was an adjustment too. Let's get right into it!

In 6th grade, I joined the Concert Band with Mr. Modia, one of the best music teachers ever. He put me in the percussion section, and I played the bells while my older brother, Matthew, played the clarinet. Band time was a ton of fun and most of my middle school memories are from it. I also joined the guitar ensemble, but I do not remember if that was in 6th or 7th grade. Either way, it was a lot of fun. When any time Mr. Modia could not make it to class for whatever reason, we would always go to PE and just have fun outside. Around this time, I met Cedric, who played percussion too, and we became friends. I also joined the robotics club run by Dr. Foote and participated with some friends in many science fair adventures, such as VEX Robotics competitions, bottle rocket challenges, and robotic hand challenges. As for my science fair project, I made one with my friend, Liam. We wanted to determine which beach had the most bacteria, North, Middle, or South. This project got us to districts in the science fair and it was super cool to experience that. We also saw the Bat Mobile too. I played on the golf team all throughout middle school and in 6th grade, I made a 30ft putt that took our team to districts (I did not even know until after I made the putt).

In 7th grade, I was in the same clubs and teams with some new friends. Life was pretty fun and busy, and music took up most of it. I basically did the same things as last year. It is hard to remember the individual years specifically from the overall section of just "middle school" so this year is going to be blander. I had a civics teacher named Ms. Lawrence, and she said she fell asleep to the sound of nails on a chalk board. She had a reputation for hating all children and kids in her class and if you were in her class, you were going to feel her wrath. One time she gave us an assignment to bring 5 pencils and 5 pens to class because she found a student did not have any. I brought my 5 pens and pencils, but my friend, Matijah, did not. So, trying to be sneaky, I handed him my pens and pencils after she checked me, and he was about to get away with it but she caught on somehow. I think either someone said something or someone else tried to do it but messed up and got caught. Either way, after we got caught, I took my pencils and pens back and Matijah got the bad grade. I still feel bad about that, but my hands were tied there. I know Matijah still remembers too, we talk about it from time to time.

In 8th grade, I did the same things as the years before. Same clubs/teams, same friends, except not entirely. In this class I had with Mr. Hickey, Jacob sat in front of me. Jacob played clarinet in the concert band and was in my grade too, we just never really talked. But we kicked it off pretty much immediately to form one of the best friendships I have ever made. That class was pretty fun, but it was way more fun playing Minecraft together after school ended. Honestly, Jacob is probably the reason I played WAYYYY too many video games in high school. Though, I would not want it any other way.

Miami Beach Senior High

I attended Miami Beach Senior High School and played on the varsity baseball and golf teams and in the jazz ensemble for four years (golf was freshman and sophomore year only). High school was something else, basically little kids thrown into a facility with practically adults. The weirdest mix of people and ages I have ever seen. Yet, it is easily the most fun I have ever had in school.

In 9th grade, I found myself with the same crew from middle school and it was us against the world. We all had classes together and had a blast. Music classes were fun, the golf and baseball teams were fun, honestly freshman year was peak. I made friends with a lot of seniors that year that I will miss after they leave. I cannot remember the last time I talked to or messaged any of them. In jazz band, Dr. Leon was the teacher. He was an interesting fellow. He was this fat Cuban guy and was very strict with playing the music right and would always yell at the class when we did it wrong. I remember hearing a story from the Concert Band that he crashed out and threw a music stand at a student for doing something wrong. Little to say he was not there the next year. I think he actually got a much better job elsewhere.

In 10th grade, it was the same story. However, we had a new music teacher, Mr. Sterne, and he was the BEST. He was personable, funny, and understood how to teach music to his students. We played a lot of great songs until that dreaded "2-week break" for COVID that turned into a year and a half of online school. Baseball was really fun too, though I did not play. But having fun is what matters most. To see my creative essay on my high school baseball experience (player's tribune style), click here. I remember when Coach Charlie told us to hand in our jerseys. It was March 13th, Friday the 13th, 2020, and we just beat Krop 5-2. Charlie told us to huddle up and to throw our jerseys in a pile because the season was cut short. He told us that this 2-week break is going to last much longer and deep down we all knew it too. That was the last time I saw our two seniors, Caleb and Anthony, while they were in High School. COVID was weird, especially the Zoom/Microsoft teams and asynchronous classes we had to end that year off. More times than not, the teachers just canceled class and gave us busy work to do on our own. Everything was shut down, so we just played games instead. I remember building a Rube Goldberg for my Honors Physics class and making a really cool video out of it. Little did I know that that would be the last assignment ever graded by my teacher, Mr. Li, as he would unfortunately take his own life over the summer.

In 11th grade, classes started and ended on the computer. Everything was on Zoom or Microsoft teams. It was like we were in a lazy student's heaven because the teachers would not know that we were not listening and were just playing games instead. This period of time had so much uniqueness, nay quirkiness about it that I cannot even begin to describe it all. But one thing I do know is that it was fun. A LOT of fun. So much slacking off was had, and sleeping in too. Because of Mr. Li's death, there was no one to teach AP Physics, so the school quickly hired Mrs. Adis Rodriguez, a super old physics teacher that had a super thick spanish accent and did not know how to use a computer. During class on Microsoft Teams, she would open a whiteboard that she could draw on to explain the material. Only issue is that ANYONE could draw on it. Now I will not say what we drew on those white boards, but if you were there, you know just how funny it was. Also, for those who were there, remember the long medium short? Now that was awesome. Eventually school hired a different teacher for the second half of the year, but I would just sleep through her classes because it was even worse than Adis'. Now for Mrs. Lemes, our AP Calculus AB teacher. I had her class at a ripe 7:20 AM and remember I woke up at 7:15 AM. Immediately she would yell into the camera: "Good morning, please turn on your cameras." This was the worst because who at 7:20 AM wants to put a bright light on their face and then a camera of said face. NO ONE DOES!!! But alas, we had to otherwise we would be marked as absent (which cannot be legal, right?). Thankfully, we did have a baseball season, but we had to wear face coverings. The one positive here is that I got a super cool, extremely limited edition MBSH Baseball neck gaiter. Man, this year was chalk full of the unexpected, and if I wanted to, I could write 10x more on it. But we need to move on!

In 12th grade, classes were back in person, and everyone made the slow transition back to real life. But something was off. People always say that nothing felt the same after COVID and they are right. People just had this, despair? maybe dread? about themselves. Everyone clearly lost something or changed drastically from being inside and online for so long. It was like we were kids anymore, like we had the thousand-yard stare. Now this is getting dark. I wrote my own personal depiction of how COVID changed us here. Besides the gloom, there was plenty of light. Socializing came back eventually and it started to feel like normal high school again. Except, I was a senior, about to leave the very place I just got to. College applications were coming up and I had no clue what I wanted to do. I tried to get recruited for baseball, but nothing came out of it. I did not know what I wanted to major in either. I think COVID ruined my creativity a bit as I just felt like there was nothing to gain from going to college. But my parents wanted me to get a degree as they told me it is a door opener for so many opportunities in life. So, I applied to plenty of schools (many of which I should never have because it was a waste of time and money, but I did not know what I was doing). I eventually narrowed my list down to FIU and UMass Amherst. If I were to go to FIU, it would have been practically free. I also had a nice scholarship for UMass Amherst. But it appeared that life had something else in mind for me. If you want to hear about that story, check my time at Penn State Fayette. Going back a little, classes, baseball, and jazz band were all way too much fun. I was Mr. Sterne's TA that year with Salima and I had so much fun organizing all the music books and playing instruments or watching movies in our free time. Nearing the end of the year, the senior trip, Grad Bash, was coming up. Essentially, all the public schools in Florida rented out Universal Studios for a night and after 6:00 PM, only high school seniors were allowed in the park. I was not planning to go on this trip as I had a baseball game the same day. But Charlie canceled the game and I could go. Only issue is, I did not have a ticket, and the trip was less than a week away. So, on bring your child to work day, Cedric concocted a plan to get me on that trip. I emailed Ms. Villoch and made a plan to talk to her about why I should go on the trip. Little to say, it worked and I was on. Grad Bash was something I planned to just have stupid kid fun at, but once again, life threw me a curveball. To read more about that, click here. My final year of high school ended on a strong note, with nothing but love for and from my friends and family with wishes for a promising future.

Penn State Fayette

For my freshman and sophomore years in college, I attended Penn State Fayette, the Eberly Campus, a Penn State University branch campus located in southwest Pennsylvania in a town called Uniontown. It was small, real small, having only 300-500 students, tops. Why did I choose to come here out of all places? Baseball. I wanted to play college baseball and get an education at the same time. I got recruited here at the end of July in the summer after I graduated high school. I went to a showcase at IMG Academy in Bradenton Florida in a last-ditch effort to get recruited. I pitched, played first base, and hit during the games there and Coach Ryan Encapara saw me and recruited me, saying that I can pitch and play first base on his team. I said yes and next thing you know I was unenrolling from FIU, applying to PSU Fayette, looking for an apartment, getting car, getting a driver's license (yes, AFTER I got the car), driving up there, and getting ready for school in a new state, city, and way of life. Miami is a densely populated area with a warm climate. Uniontown brands a remarkable 10,000 residents with four full seasons. It was like I was thrown into the deep end. But I managed to tread and swim with the help of my friends and family. By the way, I was majoring in Electrical-Mechanical Engineering Technology, hoping to become a great engineer.

I remember the very first college class I showed up to my freshman year, it was a 6:00 PM intro to electrical engineering course. The lights in the classroom were off and there were four of us there: a young-looking guy named Alex, a 32-year old super buff and tall dude named Jamie, a guy that looked like he came straight from the army, and me. We waited for a while, and nothing happened. Eventually, we managed to find his phone number on the syllabus, and I decided to call him. Ron Kalin, the professor, answered and told me that class got canceled for the first day and that he sent an email. Thing is, all of us there registered for the class after he sent the email, meaning we were not on the list. Jamie was upset because he drove 30 minutes and wasted his time, but I did not care too much because I lived 2 minutes away. That is how I met my first college friends, Alex and Jamie. Three more guys would soon join the group, first Josh, a 39-year-old with a wife and two kids, then Jacob and other Josh, both slightly older than me by a year or so. Soon, Scott joined us to make it six. The six of us would rule the engineering program at the school.

I remember how well the professors liked me (and I liked them too). They were all really great and taught me so much. Specifically, Dr. Bohna, Dr. Rasoul, Dr. Rutledge, and Dr. Angle would become the main professors I had for my engineering classes, with each professor having a large impact on my personal engineering life. When I wanted to transfer out, Dr. Rutledge was the one who recommended I switch my major to physics, as he said I was great at it in his classes. He also told me how fine of an institution Grinnell College was and that if I went there, I would do well. Dr. Rasoul was my mentor for the Launch Box project, where I developed my own prototype catheter machine. He was also the professor for two classes where I developed a robotic arm and robotic car from scratch. I know I did not talk much about baseball here, but I actually started to dislike baseball while being on the team. I liked some of my teammates, but the ones who partied crazily I did not care for. Plus, Coach Encapara did not let me play first base and broke my baseball bat without apologizing or even taking accountability, so I knew I had to transfer.

Grinnell College

And now we arrive here, at Grinnell College, a random liberal arts school no one has ever heard of in the middle of nowhere Iowa. While immensely pretty, it barks a rough 10,000 resident population and is way more rural than Uniontown. But besides the location, the school is like its own country or civilization. With a student population around 1,700, the school is rather packed full of every kind of background you could imagine. This school specifically picks its students so it can be as diverse as possible. I have met so many people here from all around the world in places I did not even know existed. Now, I transfered to for one reason and one reason only: to play college baseball with my little brother, Jacob. Yeah, the degree is great and looks well and got me into Columbia and many other schools, but I did not care for that. I wanted to play college baseball Jacob. Even though being on this team gave me far more frustration than I ever could have possibly imagined, I would not trade a moment of it for the world. Just being able to say I played on the same baseball field as my brother in college is something I never will take for granted. Besides my academic performance being decent at best, I turned this experience into one that will last for the rest of my life. And now, as I am writing this with 2 more days of classes left and conference playoffs kicking off in 3 days, I really do think it all went by fast. WAY too fast. Writing this "Education" section just made me realize how much time has passed, how many memories I have made and forgotten over the years, the names of people who I connected with but forgot, and so much more all have resurfaced in my mind and heart. None of anything that I have ever done could have been possible without every single person in my life, most importantly my parents. Now, I am not using this as a "Thank You" page for anything, it is simply a deep reflection on how far I have come and how much further I will go. Grinnell, while I may have honestly thought your school was stupid as absolute heck, it made for two of the best years of my life.
